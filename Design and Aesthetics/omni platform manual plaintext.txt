What I **can** help with is a **safe, high-end, Palantir-inspired enterprise decision platform** that:

# 1. Executive summary

Omni should be built as a **native desktop mission application** with a dense but understandable operator interface, a strong domain model, a graph-first data backbone, and a modular service architecture. Its job is not merely to display data. Its job is to:

1. ingest many authorized data streams,
2. normalize and relate them,
3. expose them through a clear ontology,
4. surface confidence-scored insights and alerts,
5. support human workflows and decisions,
6. preserve audit, provenance, and operator accountability,
7. continue operating in constrained and degraded environments.

The product should feel like a **high-control, high-context operations console**, but it must remain original in branding, layout system, and interaction details. It may be inspired by the genre of command-and-control analytics platforms, but it should not copy trade dress, proprietary workflows, or branded product identity.

### Core reframed outcome
Omni is not a “god tool” in the sense of unconstrained power. It is a **governed operations platform** that gives the right people the right view of the right data, with the right approvals, at the right time.

# 2. Design foundations from the books

This manual applies the principles from the first study guide, especially:

## 2.1 Clean Architecture
Use Clean Architecture to separate:
- enterprise rules,
- use cases,
- adapters,
- infrastructure and delivery mechanisms.

That means:
- desktop UI is a detail,
- database choice is a detail,
- model provider choice is a detail,
- connector technology is a detail,
- the core mission workflows and domain language stay stable.

### Clean Architecture implications for Omni
- The domain model cannot depend on WPF, WinUI, Qt, Electron, or any UI toolkit.
- The domain model cannot depend on a specific graph database.
- The use cases cannot depend on a specific AI vendor.
- Data connectors cannot be allowed to leak raw vendor semantics into the domain.
- Offline mode should degrade through adapters, not by rewriting core logic.

## 2.2 Domain-Driven Design
DDD matters because Omni is fundamentally a **meaning-making** product. The hard part is not drawing maps or graphs. The hard part is expressing the domain clearly enough that the system can support real decisions.

That means Omni needs:
- a ubiquitous language,
- bounded contexts,
- aggregates and invariants,
- clear ownership of models,
- explicit translation between data source terms and product terms.

## 2.3 Effective Java and API discipline
Even if much of Omni is built in C# and C++, the Effective Java mindset still applies:
- simple and explicit APIs,
- immutability where possible,
- builder patterns for complex construction,
- careful contracts,
- defensive copying at trust boundaries,
- strong exception taxonomy,
- concurrency discipline,
- versioned serialization and upgrade safety.

### In plain terms
Omni should not become an untestable pile of adapters, widgets, and async side effects. It needs a disciplined internal platform.

# 5. Product principles

1. **Human-in-the-loop by default**  
   The system recommends, ranks, and explains. A human approves consequential actions.

2. **Provenance over magic**  
   Every insight should show where it came from.

3. **Original UX, genre-informed design**  
   Dense operational UI is fine. Copying proprietary product identity is not.

4. **Graceful degradation**  
   The product must work in normal, degraded, and isolated environments.

5. **Least privilege everywhere**  
   Users, services, connectors, models, caches, and updates all operate with constrained permissions.

6. **Audit everything important**  
   Query, export, model invocation, case changes, approvals, and administrative actions all leave durable trails.

7. **Offline is a first-class mode, not a broken fallback**  
   The desktop client should still provide value when disconnected.

8. **Modularity before ambition**  
   Build separable workstreams that can be independently tested and integrated.

# 6. Ubiquitous language

Omni needs a shared vocabulary so product, design, engineering, security, and operators stop talking past each other.

## 6.1 Core terms
- **Source**: an external system or feed.
- **Connector**: code that ingests and normalizes data from a source.
- **Record**: a raw or lightly normalized datum.
- **Entity**: a durable domain object, such as a person, device, account, asset, organization, vehicle, or location.
- **Event**: something that happened at a time.
- **Observation**: a source-derived claim with provenance.
- **Case**: a bounded operational or investigative workspace.
- **Alert**: a system-generated notice requiring review.
- **Insight**: a derived relationship, pattern, explanation, or recommendation.
- **Action**: a governed workflow step initiated by a user.
- **Playbook**: a reusable sequence of review and response tasks.
- **Evidence**: a preserved record attached to a case.
- **Provenance**: the chain of origin and transformation.
- **Confidence**: a score attached to a claim or inference.
- **Snapshot**: a saved state of app version, configuration, or data cache.
- **Mode**: normal, degraded, or isolated operating state.

## 6.2 Why this matters
A source may say “subscriber,” another may say “account,” another may say “principal.” Omni should not leak each raw term upward. The domain should expose a consistent language and keep vendor-specific translation at the edges.

# 8. Domain model backbone

## 8.1 Primary aggregates

### UserSession
Fields:
- sessionId
- userId
- authMethod
- issuedAt
- expiresAt
- mode
- deviceBinding
- capabilitySet

Invariants:
- only one active privileged session per policy where required,
- session capabilities must be derived from policy, not client-side toggles,
- offline session duration must obey strict TTL rules.

### SourceDefinition
Fields:
- sourceId
- sourceType
- owner
- trustLevel
- connectorPolicy
- schemaVersion
- retentionPolicy

Invariants:
- every source has a declared owner,
- every source declares lawful basis and retention rules,
- every connector must be mapped to approved scopes.

### EntityAggregate
Fields:
- entityId
- entityType
- canonicalAttributes
- alternateIdentifiers
- provenanceSet
- confidenceProfile

Invariants:
- canonical form is derived, not arbitrary,
- merges require traceable evidence,
- unresolved identity conflicts remain explicit.

### CaseAggregate
Fields:
- caseId
- title
- status
- participants
- tasks
- evidenceRefs
- timelineRefs
- approvals

Invariants:
- evidence additions are append-only or versioned,
- closed cases cannot be modified without reopening policy,
- sensitive actions require role and approval checks.

### AlertAggregate
Fields:
- alertId
- category
- severity
- supportingEvidence
- confidence
- recommendationSet
- assignee
- ackState

Invariants:
- alerts must be explainable,
- every recommendation must link to supporting evidence or rule output,
- suppressions must be recorded with reason and actor.

# 15. Security and trust model

## 15.1 Security objectives
- authenticate strongly,
- authorize precisely,
- minimize secrets exposure,
- protect local caches,
- verify updates cryptographically,
- preserve integrity in degraded modes,
- maintain auditability.

## 15.2 Authentication
Support:
- SSO in normal mode,
- smartcard or hardware-backed auth where available,
- local sealed offline auth package for continuity mode,
- short-lived offline access tokens with strict expiry,
- explicit privilege elevation flow for sensitive actions.

## 15.3 Authorization
Use layered authorization:
- role-based access control for broad capability classes,
- attribute-based rules for case, data class, geography, and sensitivity,
- approval workflows for exports and destructive actions.

## 15.4 Break-glass design
In a severe continuity scenario, access should still be possible, but safely.

Requirements:
- pre-provisioned break-glass identities,
- dual control or approval policy where feasible,
- local cryptographic verification,
- mandatory reason entry,
- visible session banner,
- heavy audit logging,
- post-event review requirement.

Do **not** design break-glass as an easy bypass. Design it as a constrained resilience mechanism.

## 15.5 Local storage
- local encrypted cache,
- per-user protected key material,
- separate store for snapshots and rollback bundles,
- secure deletion for expired sensitive material when possible,
- offline package expiration and integrity checks.

# 16. Deployment modes

## 16.1 Mode 1: normal connected mode
Characteristics:
- central services available,
- source sync active,
- collaboration active,
- remote analytics and model inference available,
- update checks enabled.

## 16.2 Mode 2: degraded continuity mode
Characteristics:
- central services partially unavailable,
- one workstation or small server node may act as local service host,
- local graph index and local inference enabled,
- selected cached data available,
- collaboration reduced or local-only,
- update checks deferred or manual.

## 16.3 Mode 3: isolated/offline mode
Characteristics:
- no central backend assumed,
- desktop app operates on local cache, local files, and permitted public data fetched when connectivity exists,
- case work, timeline review, map analysis, document analysis, and local recommendations still function,
- no assumption of privileged remote capabilities.

## 16.4 Degradation strategy
The same use cases should degrade by:
- smaller data window,
- reduced collaboration,
- slower analytics,
- reduced model sophistication,
- explicit freshness warnings,
- cached ontology and rules.

The core workflows should remain recognizable.

# 17. Update, rollback, and tray service

## 17.1 Tray service responsibilities
- show sync and mode status,
- expose settings,
- manual check for updates,
- show update progress,
- allow rollback to prior snapshot,
- package diagnostic bundle for support.

## 17.2 Update model
1. client checks signed manifest,
2. compares version and compatibility,
3. downloads staged package,
4. verifies signature and checksum,
5. runs preflight validation,
6. installs into staged side-by-side location,
7. performs first-run health checks,
8. marks new version active if healthy,
9. preserves rollback pointer to previous stable version.

## 17.3 Rollback model
Rollback should:
- restore the last stable app version,
- preserve user workspace state where compatible,
- preserve local data caches when safe,
- produce a failure report bundle,
- defer telemetry upload until connectivity exists.

## 17.4 Checks and balances
- no unsigned update can install,
- no auto-update should activate if core health checks fail,
- rollback should be one click for authorized admins,
- incompatible local schema migrations should require explicit confirmation or staging.

- WinUI 3 or WPF
- native interop boundary for performance modules

## 22.2 Native modules
- modern C++ for high-performance processing,
- careful FFI boundary with versioned contracts,
- minimal shared mutable state.

## 22.3 Backend and services
Reasonable options:
- C# services for platform alignment,
- Java services acceptable for graph/indexing if team strength dictates,
- Python only for bounded ML/data science services, not as the whole backbone.

## 22.4 Data stores
- PostgreSQL for operational truth,
- OpenSearch or Elasticsearch for search,
- Neo4j, JanusGraph, or another graph solution depending scale and licensing,
- object storage for attachments,
- LiteDB / SQLite / embedded search locally for offline mode.

## 22.5 AI
- pluggable model provider abstraction,
- local inference path via ONNX Runtime, llama.cpp, or similar approved local runtimes,
- remote provider abstraction with strict governance.

# 27. Risks and failure modes

## 27.1 Product risk
Trying to be everything for everyone will kill coherence.

Mitigation:
- choose 2 to 3 primary mission areas first.

## 27.2 Architecture risk
Too much logic in the desktop UI will make testing and portability collapse.

Mitigation:
- keep use cases and domain model outside UI.

## 27.3 Data risk
Bad identity resolution creates false certainty.

Mitigation:
- confidence scoring, merge review queue, provenance visibility.

## 27.4 AI risk
Analysts may trust polished nonsense.

Mitigation:
- provenance requirement, confidence display, no opaque auto-action.

## 27.5 Security risk
Offline mode can become a giant exception path.

Mitigation:
- explicit offline security model, short TTLs, device binding, audit on reconnect.

## 27.6 Update risk
Bad updates can strand field users.

Mitigation:
- staged health checks, side-by-side install, rollback snapshots.

# 33. Expanded capability envelope from the original brief, kept high-level and governed

This section is included so the manual does **not disregard** major categories named in the original brief. The purpose here is to **acknowledge the full ambition at a high level** while keeping the content non-operational, lawful, and safety-bounded.

## 33.1 Product inspiration, restated safely
Omni may take inspiration from:
- the general category of dense, graph-heavy, map-heavy operational intelligence software,
- mission-control style investigation and coordination tools,
- advanced geospatial and “world view” style data fusion experiences,
- analyst workbenches that turn many inputs into human-usable decisions.

That inspiration should inform:
- information density,
- linked map/graph/timeline views,
- operator control over filters and drill-down,
- workflow-driven actionability,
- saved workspaces and reusable playbooks.

It should **not** lead to copying protected branding, proprietary visual identity, or one-for-one imitation of another product’s exact interaction model.

## 33.2 Expanded data families to account for the original brief
Omni’s architecture should be able to represent, normalize, and relate a very broad set of **authorized** data families, including but not limited to:
- documents and files,
- structured records and tabular feeds,
- GIS layers and geospatial data,
- GPS or location-derived telemetry,
- image metadata and image-derived observations,
- video and camera event streams or summaries,
- traffic, transit, mobility, and route status data,
- environmental and weather context,
- public records,
- open-source intelligence,
- cyber defense telemetry from owned or authorized systems,
- radio-frequency or signal-adjacent metadata that is lawfully available and policy-approved,
- device inventories and beacon-like identifiers where authorized,
- communications-related metadata where lawfully available,
- sensor outputs from enterprise, industrial, infrastructure, or field systems,
- operator-entered notes, annotations, and case material,
- historical archives and replayable event sequences.

### Important note on sensitive categories
The original brief named categories such as Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, SSID, LTE, and similar signals. In Omni, those are represented only at the level of **authorized signal metadata categories and lawful device/network observables**, not as instructions for collection, exploitation, interception, or unauthorized access.

## 33.3 Expanded operator outcomes to account for the original brief
The original brief emphasized that the platform should turn data into “usable actions.” Safely reframed, Omni should support high-level action families such as:
- alerting and escalation,
- case creation and triage,
- route or area review,
- watchlist or priority-list review,
- subject, asset, or incident tracking within lawful authority,
- investigation planning,
- observation or monitoring plans where legally authorized,
- security validation planning in owned environments,
- incident response and containment planning,
- logistics and deployment planning,
- deconfliction of overlapping cases or operations,
- task assignment and approval workflows,
- evidence packaging,
- report generation,
- continuity and recovery actions during outages,
- after-action review and audit preparation.

## 33.4 Interpreting sensitive terms from the brief in a safe architecture sense
Some terms from the original brief imply high-risk activity. To preserve the intent without making the manual unsafe, Omni should reinterpret them as follows:

- **"targets"** becomes **priority entities, assets, incidents, or mission objects** in a lawful operational sense.
- **"surveillance"** becomes **authorized monitoring, observation workflows, or sensor/task coordination** with legal and policy controls.
- **"pentesting"** becomes **authorized security validation, attack-surface review, or controlled assessment** of systems the user owns or is permitted to test.
- **"tracking"** becomes **governed entity, asset, or event tracking** with provenance and retention rules.
- **"actions"** becomes **human-approved workflow steps, notifications, coordination tasks, or integrations**.

This keeps the architecture aligned with the original ambition while preventing the document from becoming a guide for abuse.

## 33.5 Subject-oriented and case-oriented workflows
The original brief described monitoring people or persons of interest. In Omni, this should be represented only through a governed **Subject/Entity Monitoring** capability with the following boundaries:
- the subject can be a person, device, account, vehicle, facility, or organization,
- all monitoring must be tied to a lawful case, mission, incident, or compliance process,
- every watch condition needs provenance and approval,
- retention and access rules must be explicit,
- sensitive views should require elevated role and justification,
- alerts should remain advisory until reviewed by a human.

The architecture should therefore support:
- subject profiles,
- case-linked monitoring criteria,
- threshold-based alerts,
- timeline accumulation,
- evidence attachments,
- review queues,
- audit history,
- deactivation and archival workflows.

## 33.6 Signals, mobility, and geospatial capability families
The brief emphasized a wide range of spatial and signal-oriented data. Without getting into acquisition or exploitation specifics, Omni should support these **capability families**:
- geolocation-aware entity history,
- path and route visualization,
- proximity and co-occurrence analysis,
- area-of-interest overlays,
- movement summarization,
- temporal replay,
- anomaly detection over mobility patterns,
- lawful association of signal metadata to places, assets, or events,
- confidence-scored correlation between spatial and non-spatial evidence,
- map-linked alerting and geofenced workflows.

## 33.7 Image, video, and camera capability families
The brief named image and camera data. Omni should support this domain at a high level through:
- image or frame-level metadata ingestion,
- detections and tags produced by approved analytics,
- provenance back to source frame or clip,
- event summarization,
- timeline placement,
- entity linking where confidence permits,
- case attachments,
- human review and correction,
- retention and export controls.

## 33.8 Cyber and security-operation capability families
The brief referenced cyber-relevant data and actionability. Safely scoped, Omni can support:
- owned-network telemetry correlation,
- endpoint and asset relationship mapping,
- security alert enrichment,
- identity and access anomaly review,
- incident triage,
- containment recommendation drafting,
- defensive playbook execution with human approval,
- authorized validation planning,
- report and evidence generation for internal defenders.

It should not embed exploit chains, unauthorized access flows, or step-by-step offensive tradecraft.

## 33.9 Government and military style resilience requirements
The brief stressed that the app should be usable by highly demanding organizations and keep working during severe outages. That translates into engineering requirements such as:
- mode-aware operation,
- local compute fallback,
- local encrypted cache,
- portable continuity node support,
- intermittent sync tolerance,
- explicit degraded-mode UX,
- durable audit trail,
- cryptographically verified updates,
- rollback and snapshot restore,
- break-glass access with strong review.

## 33.10 Three-environment requirement, restated
The original brief described three scenarios. Omni should formally support them:

### Environment A: normal federated operations
- remote services available,
- shared data plane available,
- enterprise identity available,
- rich AI assistance available,
- central update and telemetry available.

### Environment B: continuity node operations
- only a single strong workstation, tower, or portable node may be available,
- some services are collapsed onto that node,
- reduced but meaningful data fusion still works,
- model serving may be local,
- sync may be delayed or one-way.

### Environment C: isolated local operations
- no central servers assumed,
- app runs from local capability pack,
- only pre-positioned data, local data, or lawfully reachable public sources are available,
- workflows continue in constrained mode,
- outputs are reviewable, exportable, and later synchronizable.

## 33.11 Action engine, kept vague and governed
The brief described implementing actions based on user input. Omni can include an **Action Engine** as long as it stays governed and non-abusive. It should support high-level action categories like:
- notify,
- assign,
- request review,
- request approval,
- open case,
- append evidence,
- generate plan draft,
- trigger internal integration,
- queue containment or response workflow,
- create a field task or checklist,
- schedule follow-up,
- mark for synchronization.

All consequential actions should be:
- role-gated,
- logged,
- reversible where possible,
- linked to reason and provenance,
- subject to policy checks.

## 33.12 Recommendation engine, kept high-level
The recommendation layer can remain ambitious while vague. It can recommend things like:
- next-best review step,
- missing data needed for confidence,
- likely related entities,
- relevant map overlays,
- useful comparison windows,
- candidate playbooks,
- likely escalation path,
- suggested response package,
- suggested continuity mode changes during outages.

The system may rank and draft. The operator approves.

## 33.13 Expanded bounded contexts suggested by the brief
To better account for the full ambition of the original request, Omni can optionally add these bounded contexts later:
- **Subject Monitoring Context** for governed watch conditions,
- **Mobility Context** for route, location, and movement abstractions,
- **Signal Context** for lawful RF/network metadata abstractions,
- **Media Context** for image/video/camera event abstractions,
- **Response Planning Context** for action packages and playbooks,
- **Continuity Node Context** for one-machine or one-node operations,
- **External Sync Context** for delayed federation and reconciliation.

## 33.14 Expanded screen ideas, still high-level
Without getting into proprietary imitation, the UI can safely include high-level screens such as:
- strategic overview board,
- live operations map,
- entity/subject profile view,
- device or asset relationship view,
- case timeline board,
- signal or telemetry context pane,
- media review pane,
- route and movement workbench,
- planning and approvals console,
- continuity health dashboard,
- update and rollback diagnostics panel.

## 33.15 High-level “god view” reframed responsibly
The original brief used “god view” language. In a safe enterprise architecture, that should mean:
- broad cross-domain visibility,
- synchronized map/graph/timeline awareness,
- rapid drill-down from overview to evidence,
- ability to coordinate many workflows from one shell,
- mode visibility across systems and operations,
- high-context rather than unrestricted power.

In other words, Omni should aim for **deep situational awareness**, not unconstrained authority.
